# Signal Scout Beta User Guide

## Purpose

Signal Scout watches cattle sale listings and sends you a WhatsApp alert when a
lot appears to match the buying criteria you submitted.

This beta is designed to test whether those alerts are useful, relevant, and
timely in real buying situations.

## What Signal Scout Does

Signal Scout is an alerting tool, not a marketplace.

It checks live sale listings against the buying brief you submitted in the beta
setup form and sends a WhatsApp message when a lot looks like it may be worth
your attention.

The aim is to help you notice relevant cattle faster, not to replace your own
judgment.

## What You Will Receive

You may receive two types of WhatsApp message:

### `WATCHING`

This means Signal Scout has noticed a potentially relevant lot, but the full
catalogue detail may not yet be available.

Treat it as an early heads-up, not a final recommendation.

### `ALERT`

This means the lot passed the current criteria checks using the information
available at the time.

Treat it as a prompt to inspect the listing, not as an instruction to buy.

## What To Do When An Alert Arrives

1. Open the listing link.
2. Check whether the lot is genuinely relevant to your buying intent.
3. Decide whether it is worth inspecting further, attending, bidding, or
   ignoring.
4. Reply if anything looks especially useful, confusing, or wrong.

## What Signal Scout Is Not

- not a replacement marketplace
- not a guarantee that every relevant lot will be found
- not financial advice
- not a recommendation to buy, bid, or transact
- not the final source of truth for lot details

The source listing remains the place to inspect, enquire, bid, or transact.

## Important Beta Notes

- This is an early beta, so some alerts will be more useful than others.
- False positives are possible.
- Missing a relevant lot is also possible.
- Your feedback is part of the product build, not an afterthought.
- If you want alerts paused or your criteria adjusted, reply directly to Tom.

## Beta Delivery Notes

Signal Scout currently uses WhatsApp delivery through Twilio.

During beta, that means:

- alerts should be treated as helpful prompts, not guaranteed operational
  notifications
- if a message does not arrive when expected, tell Tom directly
- if WhatsApp delivery becomes unreliable, Tom may pause alerts, resend, or
  temporarily use a backup communication path while the issue is fixed

If you stop receiving messages, the most helpful response is a short note such
as:

```text
I think alerts may have stopped coming through.
```

## The Most Helpful Feedback

The most useful feedback is short and practical:

- "This was relevant because..."
- "This should not have matched because..."
- "I would want to know X before acting on this"
- "I nearly missed this because..."
- "This alert made me check / attend / bid / buy"
- "This felt right, but the wording was unclear"

## Common Situations During Beta

### The alert looks relevant

That is a success signal.

Please still say so. A short reply like "relevant" or "worth a look" is useful.

### The alert looks off-target

Reply with the simplest honest reason:

- wrong sex
- wrong stage
- wrong location
- wrong weight
- not breeding stock
- too broad

### The listing looks right but the message is missing something

Tell Tom what you wanted to know before clicking through.

That kind of feedback is often more valuable than a simple yes/no rating.

### You want your criteria changed

Reply directly to Tom.

Do not wait for a formal review cycle if your buying brief has changed.

## How Feedback Will Work

Tom may check in after:

1. setup
2. your first alert
3. a real sale cycle

The simplest helpful replies are:

- relevant / not relevant
- what looked right or wrong
- whether the alert made you inspect, attend, bid, or buy

## Who To Contact

If an alert looks wrong, confusing, especially useful, or if delivery appears
to have stopped, send that feedback directly to Tom during beta.

## First-Week Ask

For the first week, the most helpful thing you can do is:

1. open the listing when an alert arrives
2. decide whether it was worth your time
3. reply briefly if it looked useful, off-target, incomplete, or if no alerts
   are arriving when you expected them

That is enough to help improve the product quickly.
